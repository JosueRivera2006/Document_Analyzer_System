Honduras, officially the Republic of Honduras, is a country located in the center of Central America with coasts on the Caribbean Sea to the north and the Pacific Ocean to the south. Honduras is a unitary state and defines itself as free, sovereign, and independent. Tegucigalpa, together with Comayagüela, form the Central District; therefore, the two cities together make up the country’s capital, being the first and largest urban center in Honduras.

Its continental territory borders the Caribbean Sea to the north and east, Nicaragua to the southeast, the Gulf of Fonseca (tributary to the Pacific Ocean) to the southwest, El Salvador to the south, and Guatemala to the west. Regarding maritime boundaries, it shares them with Mexico, Belize, Cuba, the United Kingdom through the Cayman Islands, Guatemala, Jamaica, Colombia, Nicaragua, and El Salvador. The territorial extension of Honduras, including all its islands, is 112,777 km².

The territorial organization of Honduras divides the country politically and administratively into 18 departments, which are further subdivided into a total of 298 municipalities. The form of government is republican, democratic, and representative. It is exercised through three powers: Legislative, Executive, and Judicial, which are complementary, independent, and without any subordination among them.

The population of Honduras exceeds 10 million inhabitants, mostly engaged in agricultural activities, as well as commerce, manufacturing, and public services, among other activities. The department with the highest population density is Cortés, with 400.3 inhabitants per km². The country is multiethnic. The mestizo population constitutes the majority, and there are also various indigenous peoples, such as the Lenca, Nahua, Miskito, Tolupan, Ch’orti’, Pech, Tawahka, Garifuna, English-speaking Black communities, and Hispanic-speaking Zambos and Miskito.

The territory of Honduras is very rugged; it consists of high mountain ranges, elevated plains, and deep valleys, which contain extensive and fertile plains crossed by rivers of varying flow, some of which are navigable, all of which contribute to its rich biodiversity. It is estimated that Honduras has at least 8,000 plant species, around 250 species of reptiles and amphibians, more than 700 bird species, and 110 mammal species, distributed across different regions. Some species are endemic.

Central America, of which Honduras is a part, declared its independence from Spain and the Viceroyalty of New Spain (Mexico) on September 15, 1821. After Mexico also became independent, Central America rejoined and later achieved definitive independence. In 1838, Honduras separated from Central America, and shortly after, in January 1839, the country formally adopted its first constitution. General Francisco Ferrera became the first constitutional president (1841–1843) of the country.

In 1891, the first political party, the Liberal Party of Honduras, was founded, and in 1902, the National Party of Honduras. Both parties governed the country until 2022, when the Liberty and Refoundation Party, founded in 2011, came to power. Its rise also marked the ascension of the first female president, Xiomara Castro. With the 1981 general elections and the 1982 constitution, the so-called Democratic Era began. In 2009, the country experienced its most significant political and social crisis, which included a coup d’état against the Executive Branch and fragmented the Liberal Party.

Pre-Hispanic Societies in Honduras

Before the Spanish conquest, the present territory of Honduras was inhabited by indigenous peoples characterized by two different cultural types, formed as a result of migrations that populated the American continent in the pre-Columbian era.

The Maya and Mexica cultures were governed by the so-called “Asiatic mode of production,” whose most notable feature was a sedentary economy based on agricultural production, with collective production relations centralized under a single power exercised by a set of civil, religious, and military officials. Therefore, they were not egalitarian societies. The class division was established between those who held power and the majority of the population.

Maya stela from Copán.

These cultures achieved significant progress in various fields of human knowledge, driven by the development of diverse agriculture (beans, cacao, chili, etc.) and large irrigation systems, ensuring adequate food for their large populations. They also applied highly developed techniques in textiles and ceramics. They developed intense and varied trade and achieved high scientific development in mathematics and astronomy, as well as in architecture and sculpture, which they used in constructing large cities.

In the northwestern part of Honduras, the Ch’orti’ people, a Maya group located in Copán and Ocotepeque, predominated, along with the Lenca, who extended across the departments of Santa Bárbara, Lempira, Intibucá, La Paz, Comayagua, Francisco Morazán, Valle, and part of what is now El Salvador.

The rest of the Honduran territory was inhabited by peoples from the southern continent, with nomadic and semi-nomadic cultures governed by primitive communal production relations. Among these peoples were the Tolupan (also called Xicaques), Pech (also called “Payas”), Tawahka, and Miskito, who together formed the majority of the country’s population. After the second half of the 1700s, other ethnic groups formed along the Caribbean coast: the Garifuna and English-speaking Black communities.
